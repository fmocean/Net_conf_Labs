
BGP Professional Lab 1 – govNet Edge
This lab builds a mini service-provider network called govNet that connects to an ISP and several internal regions. It’s designed to feel like a real deployment, not a toy.

Topology at a glance
10 routers total

ISP router in AS 999

govNet edge R1 in AS 1

Regional AS 23: R2, R3

Large regional confederation AS 4567:

Sub‑AS 45: R4, R5

Sub‑AS 67: R6, R7

Transit box R8 (no BGP) between R5 and R9

External customer R9 with a 4‑byte ASN

Mix of:

IPv4 + IPv6

iBGP, eBGP, confederations

GRE tunnel (R5–R9 via R8)

What problems this lab simulates
Internet edge for a government network
govNet (AS 1) connects to a Tier‑1 ISP and multiple internal ASes, just like a national network serving schools, hospitals, etc.

Multi‑AS / multi‑region design
AS 23 and confed AS 4567 model independent regions or operators that must still act as one logical backbone.

Controlled route propagation
Some prefixes should:

Stay internal only (no‑advertise)

Reach partners but not leak to the global Internet (no‑export)

Be visible only in part of the confederation (private communities)

Gradual IPv6 deployment
IPv6 is enabled on the ISP link and carried into AS 23 over existing IPv4 sessions, while other parts of the network stay IPv4‑only.

Redundancy and load‑sharing
R1 sees multiple paths (via R2 and R3) to shared prefixes and uses BGP multipath to spread traffic and survive failures.

Key technologies you configure
BGP basics

eBGP with ISP and customers

iBGP and loopbacks as router‑IDs

BGP confederation for AS 4567

Traffic engineering

Weight, local‑preference, MED

AS‑path prepending

Prefix summarization on the ISP side

Policy control

Communities: no-advertise, no-export, and custom communities (e.g., “only sub‑AS 45”)

Prefix‑lists + route‑maps for filtering and tagging

Transit‑AS blocking for specific prefixes

Route dampening for unstable routes

IPv6

Dual‑stack edge with the ISP

IPv6 over existing IPv4 BGP sessions to AS 23

Resilience / scaling features

BGP peer‑groups for cleaner configs

Multipath for equal‑cost routes

Soft reconfig / next‑hop‑self / next‑hop tracking

What you should be able to do after
By the end, you should comfortably:

Design a clean BGP edge between an ISP and a multi‑AS customer network.

Use communities and attributes to decide exactly who sees which prefixes and via which path.

Bring up IPv6 alongside IPv4 without rebuilding the whole network.

Read and write production‑style BGP configs that are modular, scalable, and debug‑friendly.
